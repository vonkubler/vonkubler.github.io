Mixed Conditionals

Why is this useful?

Mixed conditionals let you connect a past action to its present result, or a general truth to a past event. This is perfect for expressing regret, explaining current situations, or imagining different outcomes.

Combine different conditional forms to link timeframes.

➡️

If I had taken that job (past), I would be earning more now (present).

➡️

If he weren't so impulsive (present), he wouldn't have crashed the car (past).

Test Yourself

1. If she _______ harder for the exam last month, she would be at university now.

had studied

2. I _______ that house if I didn't have to commute to London every day.

would have bought

Inversion for Emphasis

Why is this useful?

Inversion adds a formal or dramatic tone. It's excellent for formal writing (essays, reports) and for making a strong, emphatic point in a speech.

Invert the subject and auxiliary verb after negative or limiting adverbs for dramatic or formal effect.

➡️

Never have I seen such a spectacular performance.

➡️

Under no circumstances should you sign that contract without legal advice.

Test Yourself

1. Little _______ that he was about to be made redundant.

did he know

2. Not until you see the data _______ how serious the problem is.

will you understand

Participle Clauses

Why is this useful?

This is a key feature of sophisticated writing. It allows you to combine ideas without simple conjunctions like 'and' or 'because', making your writing flow better.

Use participle forms (-ing, -ed, having + past participle) to create more concise sentences.

➡️

Having finished her work, she went for a run.

➡️

The report, written by the finance team, highlighted several issues.

Test Yourself

1. _______ the train was late, I arrived at the meeting on time. (Use 'despite')

Despite the train being late,

2. _______ all the necessary research, he began to write the essay.

Having done

The Subjunctive Mood

Why is this useful?

The subjunctive is for formal recommendations, demands, and hypothetical situations. The phrase 'If I were you...' is a very common subjunctive structure for giving advice.

Use the base form of a verb for demands, suggestions, wishes, or hypothetical situations.

➡️

The committee insists that the chairman resign.

➡️

It is vital that he be present at the meeting.

Test Yourself

1. The law requires that all drivers _______ insured.

be

2. I wish I _______ a bit taller!

were

Cleft Sentences

Why is this useful?

Cleft sentences are fantastic for focusing attention on specific information. They are very common in spoken English for clarifying, correcting, or adding emphasis.

Split a sentence into two parts to emphasize a specific piece of information.

➡️

It was the CEO who made the final decision, not the board.

➡️

What I find most difficult is getting up on cold, dark mornings.

Test Yourself

1. Emphasise 'the traffic' in this sentence: The traffic made me late.

It was the traffic that made me late.

2. Emphasise 'a good holiday' in this sentence: I need a good holiday.

What I need is a good holiday. / All I need is a good holiday.

Advanced Modal Verbs

Why is this useful?

These structures are essential for talking about past events when you're not 100% certain. They allow you to express different degrees of certainty, regret, or criticism.

Use modal verbs + have + past participle to speculate about the past, express regret, or make deductions.

➡️

The streets are wet. It must have rained last night. (Deduction)

➡️

I shouldn't have eaten so much cake. (Regret)

Test Yourself

1. He looks exhausted. He _______ slept well. (Express disbelief)

can't have

2. I can't find my keys. I _______ them in the car. (Express possibility)

might have left / could have left
